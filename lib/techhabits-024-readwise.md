# Tech Habits: How I Use Readwise to Think Better (Not Just Collect More)

#### For me, Readwise is not a highlights graveyard. Here’s how it earns its keep.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*G0qYZKHv6gGOs9CqtJ0yQg.png)

I’d wager that many people use Readwise like a highlights dump.

Readwise would like to have you believe that you read something interesting, you highlight it, and the app faithfully reminds you of these gems every morning through spaced repetition.

In reality, maybe you review the queue, maybe you don’t. Either way, the highlights accumulate. But the thinking doesn’t really change.

I used Readwise like that for a while. But I’ve come to believe the real power is both upstream and downstream of the highlights themselves. It’s about what you do with content around the time Readwise sees it, and what you do after you’ve read it.

This post is about that fuller picture. And it’s organized around a principle I keep coming back to from a previous installment on [Notable Folders](<https://medium.com/@geetduggal/this-one-idea-changed-the-way-i-organized-my-notes-forever-f293371a0d5b>): the well-organized mind isn’t about storing everything or bookkeeping. It’s about feeling at home in your own thinking.

In short, Readwise, its Reader app, and Obsidian integration serve as my primary tool to:

  * capture full text for various types of media, notably YouTube videos
  * consume and highlight this media
  * whittle exports for use in a broader and more memorable context

A few things worth being clear about:

This is not a system that requires constant maintenance. The automation handles the routine. You handle the intention.

It’s not about capturing everything or building an overwhelming second brain. Most things you save don’t need to go further than the queue. That’s fine.

It’s not about replacing memory. Memory often does most of the work. The system exists for what memory drops.

For me, Readwise Reader functions as the capture layer for pretty much any content I want to consume later (eg. podcasts, articles, YouTube videos, PDFs, etc). It is also the primary place where I prefer to listen to what I’ve captured. If I feel compelled to highlight something, that’s a signal that I probably would benefit from re-reading this content later.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*Wb0GWuJBZPcB4U0Xw_7cvQ.png)

The piece that makes Readwise indispensable for me is the Obsidian integration. Critically, when you set it up, your highlights**and the full text** of saved articles automatically convert to markdown and land in your Obsidian vault with appropriate YAML front matter. Not the default, generic metadata, but intentional metadata you configure to match how your system already works.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*2-v-4ZC4tWMYlUof5sOocw.png)

The upshot is that everything I’ve highlighted lives locally in Obsidian. This means this content is satisfyingly portable, offline-accessible, and efficiently retrievable. This last point is underrated. Content disappears from the internet constantly. URLs rot. Paywalls go up. Newsletters die. With this integration, what I’ve highlighted doesn’t depend on the original page surviving. It’s mine now, in a format that any future tool can read.

A quick note before going further: Readwise and Obsidian are well-known complementary tools with an existing integration. I’m not describing a jerry-rigged workaround. The point isn’t the integration itself but how to use it intentionally rather than just letting it run in the background accumulating noise you never revisit.

Perhaps here’s where most Readwise users stop. But it’s also where the real work should begin.

For me, not everything that lands in Obsidian is equal. The funnel works like this:

  1. Reader captures broadly,
  2. you consume and highlight selectively,
  3. Obsidian receives everything, and then
  4.  _you decide what actually matters._

I’ve found that memory alone works surprisingly well for most things. This fact alone challenges the need for a fancier system like this.

BUT memory has a ceiling. I often find that connections I’ve made that seemed obvious in the moment don’t survive the week.

Crucially, this system isn’t about capturing everything. It’s about having just enough structure to catch and sort what matters. I now almost gleefully accept that I’ll never read some articles I save, never highlight some articles I’ve read, and never further organize articles I’ve highlighted.

The articles that survive my whittling process are the ones that earn it through my own thought on how I want to value them, not because some automated system forced them into Obsidian. This is the subtle thing that separates a living system from a highlights graveyard.

In a previous Tech Habits post, I described ALPPS: Archives, Logs, People, Projects, Spaces. Within that structure live what I call Notable Folders, which are Wikipedia-style home bases for people, projects, and spaces that matter to me. Each notable folder is a main document with related notes and links accumulating over time.

When a Readwise highlight connects to someone I think about often, a thinker, an author, a colleague, I don’t just let it sit in the Obsidian vault. I place a link or a note directly in that person’s notable folder.

For example, I recently listened to a SOLVED podcast episode on boundaries. An intriguing note about the relationship between values and behavior caught my attention. Instead of leaving it in the general Readwise pile, I linked it into Mark Manson’s notable folder with a few comments and how it relates to a couple of other people of interest. Now that highlight isn’t a random tidbit from something I listened to once. It’s part of a growing body of thinking about how Manson (and team) approaches values and behavior, sitting alongside his books and other things I’ve noted over time.

This is the move that makes the system help me think. I am not auto-tagging everything or spending a crazy amount of effort creating atomic notes. They key is I am making a deliberate choice that this article or some content within it is stand-out _even among articles and highlights I found interesting_ , and I want it to live somewhere that will give it a longer life.

Notably (pun intended), the link to the content goes directly to my Obsidian note (which also has the full text of the podcast), **not** to the original podcast page . The page might disappear. My note won’t.

You may assume the payoff of a highlights system is better recall of facts or producing prolifically from compendium content that resonates.

But for me, the unexpectedly awesome payoff has been being more grounded in conversation with other people.

Because the highlight has been linked to a notable folder and contextualized within a broader body of my own thinking, it’s not just something I’ve just once read or some fun fact I’d mention at the water cooler.

It’s a piece of information couched comfortably in my home of thought. It has earned a place in a scaffold I’ve been cultivating for years. I know more intimately why it matters _to me_. I know how deeply how it connects to other topics of interest for me, and not in the “I’ve linked this to a thousand other notes” kinda way.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*o6DkTz2pskjNYAVbNsHAfA.png)

And as a bonus, in Obsidian, the Readwise notes render as clean local files. No loading, no app to open, and no dependency on the service being up. Often more pleasant and efficient to browse than Readwise itself!

Also, a previous Tech Habits post on [standards versus interfaces](<https://medium.com/@geetduggal/tech-habits-ditching-digital-capture-and-why-claude-hasn-t-killed-obsidian-for-me-3f912b569b37>) is worth connecting here. Here you can think of Markdown as the standard underneath both tools. Readwise and Obsidian are interfaces that conceptually sit on top of it. If either tool disappeared tomorrow, the notes remain intact and readable in any text editor.
