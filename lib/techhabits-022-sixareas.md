# Tech Habits: How I Organize My Life Into Six Areas

*Two years, six areas, and maybe a universal pattern.*

![Photo by GeoJango Maps on Unsplash](https://miro.medium.com/photos/unsplash)

Welcome to the twenty-second installment of Tech Habits, and the second in the "How I" series where I try to dig more into my lived experience. This time it's about six words that have organized my life for upwards of two years.

Here are the areas:

- Self-Care
- Spirituality
- Craft
- Discipline
- Creativity
- Relationships

These little words are small enough to hold in my working memory, which matters more than it sounds. More on that later!

First, there's a subtle but important distinction to the way I organize by area. Most systems organize by domain. Career, health, family, finances etc. Reasonable categories.

The problem is they answer "what is this?" but not "how do I relate to this?"

And the second question is the one that better matches how I work.

Consider two tasks that both 'traditionally' live under 'work' by domain: software system architecture and filing expense reports. In my system these live in completely different areas. Architecture, when I'm in flow and loving the problem, belongs in Craft. Expense reports, which I respect but would not mourn if they ceased to exist, belong in Discipline.

The domain is the same. My relationship to the work is not.

This means the same kinda task can belong to different areas depending on various things going on with myself and in my life. To illustrate this idea, writing stories for pure fun to share with a select few may be Creativity. Writing stories professionally, on deadline, for pay, starts bleeding into Discipline or Craft even if you still love doing it. The area reflects mood and relationship to the work, not just what the work is.

The strength: clustering by relationship keeps you in the right headspace. Craft mode? Pull out the notebook, headphones, and keyboard. Discipline mode? Move through the necessary stuff efficiently.

The weakness: it's subjective in the sense that an alien visiting earth may have a hard time figuring out why you put a project related to going to the gym in Creative. To compound this, your relationship to project may shift over time. Retrieval can occasionally get murky. This is decidedly not in the spirit of Johnny Decimal.

BUT the cognitive benefit of admitting how I relate to a project far outweighs the satisfaction of placing it in a well-established domain.

## The 3x2 Grid

For me, the six areas aren't a conceptually flat list. They form a 3x2 grid where each row is a pair addressing something fundamental.

```
┌─────────────┬─────────────────┐
│  Self-Care  │   Spirituality  │  ← Self
├─────────────┼─────────────────┤
│    Craft    │   Discipline    │  ← Security
├─────────────┼─────────────────┤
│  Creativity │  Relationships  │  ← Others
└─────────────┴─────────────────┘
```

### Row 1: Self

Self-Care is restoration and immediate pleasure. Things you do for enjoyment without needing to wrestle with anything: a show, a walk, a bookstore visit, exercise that feels like play.

Spirituality is where the deeper work lives. Meaning, purpose, equanimity, the inner life, and faith if it applies. This area holds things like: notes on philosophy, reflections on what moves you in stories, even the ongoing work of managing excited mental states and staying grounded. For me, these live in Spirituality rather than Self-Care because navigating your inner life is fundamentally different from watching something fun on a Friday night.

For example, consider a historical drama for pure entertainment (Self-Care), vs a story about sacrifice and nobility that moves you and change how you think about your own life (Spirituality). Same general activity on the surface. Different relationship to it entirely.

### Row 2: Security

Craft is generative security. The work you love that the world also happens to reward you for. For me, this means debugging systems, building tools and delightful products, solving engineering problems, and managing teams toward a meaningful vision. Security emerges as a byproduct of engagement, not the goal itself.

Discipline is protective security. Necessary stewardship. The things you respect enough to maintain even though, if you are being honest, you would rather not have to think about them. Paying bills, doctors appointments, etc. would fit in this category. Same with administrative work in your job that keeps the lights on.

Discipline is not a shame category. Filing something there is a declaration: "I am the CEO of my own life and this needs tending." For me the world is a different place when I become clear on that with myself. Craft produces security as a byproduct of engagement. Discipline maintains security through stewardship. Neither is optional.

### Row 3: Others

Creativity belongs in this row because creative work is not just about creating for myself. For me, I usually am internally pushed to create when I am inspired enough that I can't help but to share my experiences with others. Even if one other person is inspired, that is enough.

Relationships is direct kinship. Family, close friends, meaningful collaborations. The people you invest in.

## Filing things into areas

Most of the time, placing something in the right area is fast. A note on the architecture of a specific system at work fits well in a project with in my Craft area. A project dedicated to cleaning up old administrative work tickets goes in a 'Work employment' folder within my Discipline area. A note about someone who moved me philosophically goes in their folder in my Spirituality area.

(Note that in the spirit of Johnny Decimal, nothing goes directly into a broad area. Everything lives in a specific folder within a category within an area. For obvious captures this takes a few seconds.)

The interesting case is when there may be ambiguity or I'm determining the area for some piece of content for the first time. Here, the act of choosing is itself clarifying. Ask "how do I relate to this right now?" and an answer usually surfaces quickly.

Seasonal reviews are where the grid earns its keep. Six areas is small enough to mentally scan in about ten seconds. The uncertainty resolves fast because the option space is tight.

Within each area, I use a consistent subcategory structure from my ALPPS framework: People, Projects, and Spaces. People in Craft are colleagues I primarily collaborate with on technical work. People in Relationships are those I invest in personally. A Space in Spirituality might be an ongoing philosophical inquiry. A Space in Craft might be a technical domain I am developing mastery in. Same labels across all six areas, different meaning depending on context.

For me, the six areas themselves have been stable for two years. The People-Projects-Spaces subcategory structure is more recent, roughly the last six months. The areas came first through lived experience. The subcategory structure came later as a way to give each area a consistent internal shape.

## Working memory wins

The fact that the six areas fit in working memory sounds trivial, but IMHO it is not.

When walking to a meeting with a sudden idea, I can very efficiently place it in the right Area using the scaffold already residing in my mind. During a seasonal review, I can run through all six options in my head in seconds.

Frameworks with twelve or fifteen categories lose this. They add friction exactly where you want none. Six is enough to cover everything and small enough to know by heart.

On the other extreme, there is a growing understanding and even acceptance that you can rely on AI for most categorization and retrieval. I think there is a lot of utility to this idea, but I firmly maintain that a well organized mind brings you something greater than retrievability and not having to think about organization. It brings clarity, peace, and most importantly a space for your mind to play.

## The categories are surprisingly universal

About a year into using these areas, I came across [this post](https://medium.com/@ellane) from the one and only Ellane W, Carl Pullein's Areas of Focus workbook, and Zig Ziglar's Wheel of Life.

Pullein's eight areas: Family and Relationships, Career and Business, Health, Spirituality, Personal Development, Life Experiences and Lifestyle, Finances, Purpose in Life.

Ziglar's seven: Career, Family, Financial, Mental, Physical, Spiritual, Personal.

Both arguably organize by domain rather than relationship to work. But look at the territory covered and you see spiritual similarity my six. For example, Pullein's "Career and Business" plus "Personal Development" may map into my Craft and Discipline. His "Purpose in Life" and "Spirituality" merge into my single Spirituality area. The labels differ because they reflect different personalities. The underlying territory is quite consistent.

Ziglar has been digging into this area for forty+ years. Pullein built an entire course and workbook around it. They arrived at similar structures independently. That convergence is worth sitting with. It suggests these areas may not be arbitrary. They reflect something real about how humans organize.

What differs in my version is less the areas themselves and more the organizing principle. "How do I relate to this?" rather than "what domain is this?" produces a system that mirrors how you actually live rather than how you would describe your life on a resume.

If you try to find your areas, I'm sure they will look different from mine. Craft and Discipline will not mean the same things to you that they mean to me. Maybe you need seven, or four.

But organizing by relationship to the work rather than domain is worth trying. The act of placing something in an area forces a moment of self-assessment that sorting into an arbitrary folder you created ages ago, tagging, and linking never quite require. For me, over two years, that small repeated assessment has made this system both more useful and more interesting to live and play inside.
