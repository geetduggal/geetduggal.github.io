Tech Habits — How I Use My Pocket Bullet Journal as a Pocket Atlas

Kicking off a new “How I” series, and how I’m further torturing the Atlas Journaling metaphor

I’ve spent a lot of time in this series writing about tools, methods, and principles. Johnny Decimal, Atlas Journaling, daily highlights, the Plain Text Suite, the list goes on. What I’ve written less about is how these things actually work together on any given day: how I move through a morning, how I decide what to capture and where, how the physical and digital sides of my system stay connected . So I’m starting a new thread within Tech Habits: posts that begin with “How I.” It’s meant to provide a deeper walk-through of some part of my actual current workflow, with the hope that a piece of it sparks something useful for you! My system is specific to me. But I’ve found that even idiosyncratic workflows contain transferable ideas. This first one is about something I’ve been double-clicking on lately: using my Pocket Bullet Journal as what I’ve started calling a Pocket Atlas.

Source: James Cummins Bookseller

Part of me yearns for the days where people carried small field notebook-sizish things that compressed the essential geography of the world into something you could slip into a coat pocket. Check this out: The American Military Pocket Atlas was issued to British high command during the Revolutionary War era because it contained the essential topographical information they needed in the most convenient form possible. Also makes a good $14,500 birthday present.

A pocket atlas is like compressed atlas: you keep useful information without loss of overall structure. You can have an index. You still have the essential maps and the key reference information. A full atlas lives on a desk or reading stand, sprawling and detailed. The pocket version is its companion: a faithful but compressed version of the same essential structure, indexed so you can orient yourself quickly and move on. It doesn’t try to replace a full atlas. The writer Philip Pullman has described cherishing his childhood pocket atlas specifically because it let him imagine faraway places while remaining a reliable reference he could hold in his hands. This is spiritually what I aim for in my ‘Pocket Atlas Journal’ (PAJ).

Let me walk you through a made-up example of how it works.

First, when you open a fresh PAJ, the first thing you do is set up the index. It takes five minutes. You list the main territories you’re navigating: the projects you’re actively building, the people you’re deeply collaborating with, the spaces you’re investing time in. For this example, my index might look like:

- Project: Dashboard Redesign  
- Project: Backend Refactor  
- Person: Alex (colleague)  
- Project: Writing project

In my actual pocket bullet journal it would look more like this:

The broad areas of my life are represented as boxes in 2D with pointers to content.

That index is your visual atlas. Each entry is a territory you can navigate to. It orients every capture that follows.

Now pretend it’s a Tuesday morning. I wake up, get ready, and while I’m getting dressed, I spend maybe thirty seconds thinking about what I want the day to be about. Not a rigid plan. Just: what’s one thing I’d like to be true about today? I may jot it in my pocket journal as the daily highlight at the top of a fresh page along with a few quick things I know I want to do.

As the day unfolds, I take a few notes:

2026-02-03  
  
o Meeting with team on design feedback  
  - Alex mentioned timeline constraints  
  * Post-meeting: review wireframes for next sprint  
- Idea: Batch similar tasks

That’s rapid logging. Bullets for tasks or actions, dashes for notes. When a capture relates to something I’m tracking deeply like the Dashboard Redesign project, which already has a collection in this notebook, I annotate it. But notice: Alex gets an annotation even though there’s no Alex collection here. That’s a line item. Alex has a notable folder in my Obsidian system, but I’m not doing sustained work with him in this notebook. I’m just capturing a thought to reference later.

Backend refactor - Consider caching strategy  
                 * Request performance baseline from Sam

Sam doesn’t get an annotation. He’s a colleague, but there’s no active collaboration happening in this notebook that warrants tracking. It’s just a note.

Now consider this: I’ve been working on a significant project, the Dashboard Redesign, for the past three weeks. This has a dedicated collection in my pocket journal. Multiple pages. Sketches. Design decisions. When I have a new thought about it, I flip to that collection and add to it:

[Dashboard Redesign Collection - Page 10]  
- Layout approach: Three-column with flexible sidebar  
  - Reduces clutter, improves scannability  
  - Need to validate with user testing  
- Color palette options: review with design team Thursday  
- Blocked: waiting on API response times from backend team

This is sustained work. It has a collection. New captures go there naturally.

At the end of the day, I photograph the pages I’ve written. I create a unique note in Obsidian for that day, timestamped with the date and time, and tag it as a pocket journal entry. The photo goes there. This unique note becomes the bridge between the physical page and the digital system.

From there, during a natural ending point for a few notes in my journal or in a weeklyish review, I scan through these notes. If they are substantial enough the line items with annotations get pulled into their respective notable folders. A notable folder for a topic of significant interest is a primary document together with potentially many notes related to it.

The Monthly Log as Temporal Anchor

One more piece worth showing. At the start of each month, the pocket journal gets a monthly log spread. Not a detailed calendar. Just a brief entry for each day. Something like:

[Monthly Log - Feb 2026]  
1 (Sun) - Rest day, read about design systems  
2 (Mon) - Deep work on dashboard, productive morning  
3 (Tue) - Team meeting, started redesign sketches  
...

Each line is a few words. Maybe my daily highlight, maybe what actually stood out. Over a month, the monthly log becomes a compressed record of the recent weeks I’ve lived through: something I can hold in your hands and scan without opening any app. This mirrors the daily highlights in my digital calendar, where the same highlights populate my year view. The pocket journal and the digital system are telling the same story from different angles.

To dig a little deeper let me explain both Atlas and Bullet Journaling and in a little more depth.

Bullet Journaling , developed by Ryder Carroll, provides the syntax and mechanics. Rapid logging: short-form notation using bullets to capture tasks (•), events (○), and notes ( — ) quickly. Signifiers add context at a glance. The index maps topics to page numbers. Collections group related content. And the monthly log is not just a calendar. It’s a running record: a brief note or daily highlight for each day, a compressed view of the month’s texture you can take in at a glance. Bullet journaling gives you the capture mechanics without imposing rigid rules.

Atlas Journaling , which I’ve written about in detail, adds an organizational philosophy on top of those mechanics. It’s about understanding where things fit in a broader picture. It’s about mapping your priorities, projects, and truths spatially, and connecting your physical captures back to your digital system in a coherent way. You could think of it as bullet journaling with an additional layer of intentional connection to your digital life.

For me, the digital half lives in Obsidian, organized around the ALPPS framework: Archives, Logs, People, Projects, and Spaces. Archives are the Johnny-Decimal-inspired home for all long-lived and/or mature notable folders. Logs are working memory: daily notes and in-progress captures. People are significant individuals you track over time. Projects are active initiatives with reasonably well defined endpoints. Spaces are ongoing areas of interest (think Wikipedia article titles) without a defined endpoint. Each of these has what I call a notable folder: a dedicated note that serves as the home base for that entity. When I annotate “Alex” in my pocket journal, I’m pointing toward Alex’s notable folder in Obsidian. When I annotate “Dashboard Redesign,” I’m pointing toward that project’s notable folder.

The Pocket Atlas has three core pieces.

The Index is your atlas. When you open a fresh pocket journal, the first thing you do is set up the index with anchor topics: key people you’re working with, active projects, spaces you’re spending time in. Just what’s relevant to you while this pocket notebook is alive.

Collections are themed groupings within the same notebook. As opposed to keeping separate notebooks for specific topics, you keep dedicated collections in the Bullet Journal sense in one pocket journal. A collection for a current project. Running notes on a person. Ideas for a writing piece. Each collection is indexed, so you can flip to it quickly. For me the beauty is that these collections mirror my digital ALPPS structure. A pocket collection for a project connects to that project’s notable folder in Obsidian. A collection for a person connects to their note.

Annotations are the lightweight pointer system. Every capture defaults to a plain rapid-log entry: a dash for a note, a bullet for a task. But when it relates to one of your indexed collections, you add a brief annotation to the left margin: just the name of the person, project, or space. This takes three seconds and creates the connection without interrupting the flow of capture. At a glance, you can see how your daily captures connect to your broader system.

In my system, the pocket journal is not the source of truth. The digital system is. This is liberating. It means I’m not trying to keep two parallel systems in perfect sync. The pocket journal is a capture and reflection layer: a tactile, immediate companion that goes everywhere. Whatever you capture there eventually feeds back into Obsidian, which is where things live permanently, become searchable, and connect to everything else.

The syncing isn’t daily and it isn’t rigid. In Atlas Journaling, you think in full seasons: weeks to months. In the Pocket Atlas, you think more in terms of ‘mini seasons’ : usually anything from a day to a week. When a natural endpoint arrives, ie. a collection that feels complete, a week that wraps up, you photograph pages and bring captures into Obsidian. The photo creates a mirror between physical and digital. The same daily highlight appears in both places, in different forms.

The physical page feels more permanent in the moment. Writing something down makes it feel real in a way typing doesn’t. But the digital system has actual longevity. It’s searchable, linkable, scales with complexity. The pocket journal handles what the phone handles poorly: slow thinking, sketching, captures that benefit from the physical act of writing. The digital system handles everything else. For me, they’re doing different jobs.

In the end, I’m just so grateful to have this notebook in my every-day-carry. It really is a compliment to my digital world and keeps me grounded throughout the day. ‘Till next time! 🏔️

A few brief additional notes:

On single-purpose notebooks : Cal Newport and others have written and spoken compellingly about dedicated notebooks for single projects or topics. I’m very much on board with that idea. However, the Pocket Atlas is different. It keeps everything in one portable notebook, organized by collection and index, rather than split across multiple notebooks. This is especially useful for topics/subjects where you need something like 1–10 A5ish sized pages but not much more.

On the full-sized journal : The Pocket Atlas practice hasn’t replaced my full-sized Bullet Journal. It’s clarified what it’s actually for: big seasonal spreads, two-dimensional mapping, reflective work that asks for more physical space and maybe even a desk.
